Program Purpose

RS&H is extending its Oracle Fusion Cloud program to cover the full suite — ERP (Financials, Projects, Procurement), EPM (Planning, Consolidations, Reporting), and CX (Sales, Service, Marketing) — on the Oracle Redwood experience platform. The 7-week scope extension with the current SI (Hackett Group) was authorized to complete five foundational planning workstreams that represent known risk areas for enterprise Oracle implementations. This document synthesizes all five workstreams into a single integrated reference for the RS&H leadership team and the incoming primary SI.

Why This Investment Is Critical

Oracle Fusion implementations at firms the size of RS&H (1,500–3,000 associates) fail most commonly not because of technology gaps, but because of insufficient upfront planning. Industry data from the Hackett Group Oracle Benchmarking Program shows that AEC firms that complete deployment strategy, resource modeling, and risk assessment before SI engagement are 38% less likely to experience cost overruns exceeding 20% of contract value. Conversely, firms that enter SI negotiations without a deployment scenario decision experience an average of 2.4 contract amendments in Year 1 — each carrying a 6–14% cost premium.

The 5 scope additions documented here are not optional enhancements — they are the architectural decisions that the primary SI will use to scope their contract. Entering that contract without these decisions made is the single highest-risk action RS&H could take.

Program Recommendation Summary

  • Recommended Scenario: Scenario B — ERP first (Financials + PPM), then EPM (PBCS + FCCS), then CX (Sales + Service + CPQ). 34-month total program horizon.
  • Primary SI: Deloitte or PwC for ERP/EPM core; Hitachi or Huron for EPM specialist depth; Broadpin for OIC technical integration.
  • Deployment Model: Phased with shared COA design session in Month 1. ERP Wave 1 go-live at Month 12–14. EPM Wave 2 go-live at Month 22–24. CX Wave 3 go-live at Month 32–34.
  • Resource Commitment: 9–11 RS&H FTEs at various time commitments across the program. Controller and Finance Lead are the highest-risk resource constraints.
  • Platform: Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) tenancy, Oracle Integration Cloud (OIC) for all third-party integration, Oracle Fusion Analytics Warehouse (FAW) for cross-pillar reporting post Wave 1.

Critical Success Factors

Executive Sponsorship

CFO and COO must remain active sponsors — not delegators — through go-live. The Steering Committee must meet monthly and have authority to resolve cross-functional resource conflicts. Oracle programs without active C-suite engagement experience 3x longer defect resolution cycles.

COA Decision Lock

The Chart of Accounts design is the single most important technical decision in the program. It must be finalized by Month 2 and frozen before the EPM workstream begins. Late COA changes are the #1 cause of Wave 2 delays in Oracle ERP+EPM programs.

SI Contract Discipline

The primary SI contract should be fixed-price for Wave 1 design/build, with clearly defined RS&H SME availability commitments. Scope change requests must route through the Program Director and be approved by the Steering Committee before SI work begins.

OCM as a Workstream

Organizational Change Management must be funded as a dedicated workstream — not embedded in PMO. RS&H's finance function will experience the largest day-one change (replacing current ERP workflows entirely). Training completion rates below 85% before go-live are a go/no-go gate.

Integration Architecture First

OIC integration design must begin in Month 2 — not after ERP go-live. ADP payroll, banking feeds, and project legacy data migration are long-lead items. Integration testing requires 6–8 weeks before UAT and cannot be compressed.

Analytics from Day One

Oracle Fusion Analytics Warehouse (FAW) should be provisioned in the OCI tenancy at program kickoff. Post-Wave 1, FAW delivers pre-built ERP KPIs (DSO, AP aging, project margin) without custom BI development. This is the fastest path to executive visibility into Oracle data.

Week 1 — Foundation Setup & Current-State Alignment

Week 2 — Oracle Deployment Strategy (Scope Item 1)

Analyze and decide on the deployment scenario that governs the entire program timeline, SI scope, and organizational change plan. The scenario decision is the single most upstream dependency in the program — all subsequent workstreams (resource model, risk register, governance design, support model) are contingent on it. This week ends with a signed executive decision, not a recommendation for further review.

Key Activities

  • Scenario scoring workshop (Monday 2hrs) — Facilitate scoring of all 4 deployment scenarios (Big Bang, ERP→EPM→CX, ERP+CX→EPM, EPM First) against 6 weighted dimensions: organizational change capacity, budget constraint, time-to-value, technical complexity, AEC implementation precedent, and FDI/analytics activation timeline.
  • AEC precedent research — Pull Hackett benchmarks for firms of comparable size (1,500–3,000 employees) and industry (AEC/engineering services) that have completed Oracle Fusion. Identify which scenario was most common, what the average timeline deviation was, and which scenario produced the highest Year-1 user adoption scores.
  • RS&H-specific constraint analysis — Map each scenario against known RS&H constraints: Controller availability for Finance go-live, PDO sign-off availability for Projects go-live, CX/BD team's Salesforce contract expiration timeline, ADP integration complexity for HCM phasing.
  • FDI analytics readiness analysis — Evaluate when FDI for ERP and FDI for CX can be activated under each scenario. Big Bang enables Day-1 cross-pillar analytics; phased scenarios delay cross-pillar FAW value by Wave count × average wave duration.
  • High-level timeline build — For the top 2 scenarios (by scoring), build a month-by-month high-level timeline from contract execution to go-live, including key milestones (design freeze, data migration cutover, UAT, parallel run, go-live, hypercare end).
  • Executive decision meeting (Thursday 1.5hrs) — Present scenario scoring, RS&H constraint analysis, AEC precedent data, and recommended scenario with rationale. Obtain signed executive decision. If consensus is not reached, escalate to COO/CFO for tiebreaker — no extensions.
  • Module sequencing map — Once scenario is decided, document the Oracle module activation sequence: which modules go live in which wave, dependencies between modules (e.g., ERP COA must be finalized before EPM chart of accounts can be configured), and Oracle environment requirements per wave.

Deliverables (due by EOW Friday)

  • Scenario scoring matrix (all 4 scenarios × 6 dimensions, weighted)
  • AEC precedent analysis (3–5 comparable firm case studies from Hackett benchmark database)
  • RS&H constraint map per scenario (go/no-go assessment for each)
  • FDI activation timeline comparison across scenarios
  • High-level program timeline for selected scenario (month-by-month, 34 months)
  • Signed Deployment Scenario Decision document (Executive Sponsor + CFO + CTO)
  • Module sequencing map with dependency analysis

Week 3 — Program Resource Model (Scope Item 2)

Build the staffing model that defines every RS&H internal role required to execute the Oracle program. The resource model is the mechanism by which leadership commits to providing the people the SI needs. Without it, the SI contract will include unfavorable assumptions about RS&H availability, and contract amendments will follow. This week's output is a binding internal commitment, not an estimate.

Key Activities

  • Role inventory by workstream — For each of the 5 functional workstreams (Finance/ERP, Projects/PPM, HCM, CX/BD, Technology/OIC), identify every RS&H role required from design through hypercare. Use the 45-role model in the Resource Model tab (Program Operations) as baseline. Validate each role against the selected deployment scenario's timeline.
  • FTE commitment calibration — For each role, define: % of working time committed per month, which specific months (by wave phase), whether the role can be combined across workstreams, and whether the individual serving that role can maintain their current operational responsibilities at reduced capacity or requires backfill.
  • Named resource identification — For every high-criticality role (Program Director, Finance Lead, Projects Lead, Technology Lead, OCM Lead), confirm the named individual who will fill the role. Do not leave high-criticality roles as TBD at the end of Week 3.
  • Backfill requirement analysis — For roles with 60%+ program commitment, assess whether backfill is operationally required. Calculate backfill cost (fully-loaded rate × duration). Prepare a backfill recommendation for CFO/COO approval with three options: hire backfill, defer program, or accept risk.
  • COE staffing design — Define the post-go-live internal Center of Excellence team. Which roles transition from program roles to permanent COE roles? What is the minimum viable COE staffing for each supported pillar? When does Oracle hand off support to the internal team?
  • Resource model vs. SI assumptions alignment — Cross-check the RS&H resource model against Hackett's standard SI assumption sheet (number of SME days per workstream, availability for workshops, UAT participation). Identify any gaps where RS&H commitment falls below SI minimum expectations and escalate before SI contract negotiation.
  • Resource model financial summary — Summarize fully-loaded internal resource cost (base + benefits, not including backfill) as a program cost component. This feeds directly into the TCO model and the executive budget approval.

Week 4 — Risk Assessment & Program Risk Register (Scope Item 3)

Systematically identify, rate, and develop mitigation plans for all risks that could cause cost overrun, schedule delay, or program failure. The risk register built this week is not a bureaucratic exercise — it is the primary input to the Steering Committee's oversight agenda for the 34-month program. Every identified risk must have an owner and a mitigation plan before Week 4 closes.

Key Activities

  • Risk identification workshops (Monday–Tuesday) — Run structured risk identification sessions with each workstream. Use the Hackett Oracle risk taxonomy (15 risk categories: Resource, Technology, Data, OCM, Vendor, Scope, Integration, Regulatory, Timeline, Budget, Executive, PMO, Support, Security, License) as the framework. Each workstream identifies risks specific to RS&H's context.
  • Risk rating calibration — For each identified risk, assign Probability (1–5) and Impact (1–5) using the 5×5 risk heat map methodology. Calibrate ratings across workstreams to ensure consistency (e.g., a "High" probability in Finance should mean the same as in Technology).
  • Resource risk deep-dive — Apply special scrutiny to resource risks (the most common Oracle program failure mode). For RS&H specifically: Controller single-point-of-failure risk, Technology team Oracle Cloud skills gap, Projects SME availability during active project delivery, CX/BD organizational change acceptance risk.
  • Integration risk assessment — Evaluate the 15+ integrations identified in Week 1. For each, assess: complexity (simple API vs. complex middleware), RS&H's current internal capability to build/maintain it, Broadpin's capacity and rate, and timeline risk if an integration is delayed past go-live.
  • Mitigation plan development — For every High and Critical risk, develop a specific mitigation plan: what actions reduce the probability or impact, who owns those actions, when they must be completed, and what the residual risk is after mitigation is applied.
  • Risk register baseline & ownership assignment (Wednesday) — Finalize risk register with 15–20 identified risks, assign an owner from the RS&H leadership team to each risk. Owner is accountable for monitoring and executing the mitigation plan.
  • Steering Committee risk review cadence — Define how the risk register will be reviewed in Steering Committee meetings: which risks get standing agenda time, what escalation triggers mandatory emergency session, and how risk status is reported to the Board if required.
  • CFO, BDO, PDO risk review (Thursday) — Present financial risks, business development risks, and project delivery risks specifically to CFO, BDO, and PDO. Obtain their risk acceptance signatures for risks in their functional domain before Week 4 closes.

Week 5 — Governance Roles & RACI (Scope Item 4)

Design the governance model that will operate for the full 34-month program. Oracle Fusion programs fail most often from decision-making delays, not technology problems. A formal governance structure — with explicit accountabilities, meeting cadences, and escalation paths — is a prerequisite for the SI contract. The SI will insert its own governance overhead if RS&H has not already defined it.

Key Activities

  • Governance structure design (Monday) — Define all governance bodies: Executive Steering Committee (ESC), Program Leadership Team (PLT), Workstream Lead forums (Finance, Projects, HCM, CX/BD, Technology), and SI/Vendor coordination meetings. For each body, define: membership, meeting frequency, quorum requirements, and decision authority.
  • ESC charter drafting — Write the Executive Steering Committee charter. Members: COO (Chair), CFO, CTO, BDO, PDO (rotating), HR Director (HCM waves), Hackett PM (observer). Authority: approve scope changes >$50K, approve go/no-go gates, approve program budget changes, receive escalated issues from PLT.
  • RACI matrix construction (Tuesday–Wednesday) — Build the full RACI for 35+ decision types across all program phases. Key decision categories: Architecture (who approves design decisions), Data (who approves migration rules), Configuration (who approves Oracle configuration vs. customization), Change management (who approves org change impacts), Go-live gates (who has veto authority), Vendor management (who manages SI and Broadpin contracts).
  • Decision log framework — Define the program decision log: how decisions are documented, what level of detail is required, who has access, how long decisions are binding, and the process for revisiting a closed decision (requires ESC approval for any decision >$25K impact).

Week 6 — Oracle Support Model Design (Scope Item 5)

Design the organization's long-term Oracle Cloud support model. Post-go-live support failures are among the top three causes of AEC firm productivity loss in Year 1 of Oracle Cloud operations. Oracle's quarterly mandatory update cadence, the complexity of multi-pillar configuration management, and the Oracle Support portal's learning curve all require a deliberate, pre-planned support structure — not one assembled reactively after go-live.

Key Activities

  • COE vs. AMS decision framework (Monday) — Evaluate three support models: (1) Internal COE only — RS&H builds and staffs full in-house capability, (2) AMS partner only — outsource all post-go-live support to an Oracle AMS provider, (3) Hybrid — internal COE for functional support with AMS partner for technical/integration support. Use RS&H headcount, budget, and skills data from Week 3 resource model as input.
  • AMS partner market assessment — Evaluate the 3–4 shortlisted AMS providers against RS&H's requirements: Oracle Fusion expertise depth, AEC/engineering sector experience, OIC/integration support capability, cost model (FTE-based vs. ticket-based vs. retainer), geographic coverage for RS&H's distributed workforce.
  • Oracle Support tier selection — Compare Oracle Premier Support vs. Extended Support on: included services, response SLAs, update advisory services, Oracle Advanced Customer Services (ACS) add-ons, and cost difference. Document recommendation with cost/capability analysis.
  • COE staffing model — For the recommended support model, define: how many COE FTEs are needed per pillar (Finance, Projects, HCM, CX, Technology/OIC), what Oracle certifications are required, what the ramp timeline is for COE readiness (COE must be 80% staffed before go-live), and what the Year 1–3 COE budget is.

Deployment Scenarios

Four deployment scenarios were evaluated against RS&H's organizational capacity, risk tolerance, budget constraints, and time-to-value requirements. Each scenario is scored across six dimensions.

Scenario A: Big Bang — All Three Pillars to a Single Go-Live Date

Summary: ERP (Financials, Projects, Procurement), EPM (PBCS, FCCS), and CX (Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, CPQ) all reach production simultaneously on a single cutover date. The program runs all three workstreams in parallel from Month 1. Typical horizon: 22–26 months.

Within the Big Bang model, three distinct internal sequencing approaches exist — differing in which pillar drives architecture decisions, sets the pace, and receives prioritized resources when trade-offs arise. Select the sub-approach before program kickoff; attempting to change mid-program is highly disruptive.

A1: CRM/CX-First Big Bang

Philosophy: CX (Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, CPQ, Eloqua) is treated as the architectural anchor. All other module design decisions — customer master, contract structure, account hierarchy — are resolved first to satisfy CX requirements, and ERP/EPM adopt the CX-defined data model rather than the reverse.

Go-Live: All three pillars simultaneously at Month 22–26, but CX build begins in Month 1 and sets the pace. ERP and EPM design workshops are scheduled to not conflict with CX data model decisions.

Best For: Organizations where the primary pain point is Business Development — where the CEO/COO has a mandate to improve pipeline visibility, win rates, and client relationship management before or equally alongside financial system improvement

CX-First — Underlying Components & Prerequisites

  • Customer Master Design (CX-led): The Account/Contact/Hierarchy model in Oracle Sales Cloud is the master definition; AR Customer in ERP is a downstream subscriber. This is the reverse of ERP-first. Requires Finance to accept CX-defined customer segmentation rules.
  • Opportunity Stage Model: AEC opportunity stages (Identify→Qualify→Capture→Propose→Select→Award) must be finalized in Month 1–2 before any ERP project structure can be designed — because the CX-to-ERP project handoff data flow depends on these stages
  • CPQ Fee Structure (Month 1–3): Oracle CPQ pricing configuration requires RS&H's full rate card, fee structure rules, and scope-of-services taxonomy to be defined before ERP billing rules are configured — CPQ outputs become ERP Project Contract inputs
  • Oracle Eloqua Campaign Architecture: Marketing segmentation model (client type, geography, service line, procurement vehicle) defined early; feeds into both CX contact management and ERP customer categorization
  • Identity & SSO Configuration (Month 2): Oracle Identity Cloud Service (IDCS) configured for CX user authentication before ERP and EPM — since CX has the broadest user population (BD Directors, Marketing, all Principals)
  • CX-to-PPM Integration Blueprint (Month 3): The Opportunity→Project creation automation is architected in Month 3 to ensure both CX and ERP build teams are designing toward compatible data structures from the start

CX-First — Advantages

  • Business Development pipeline is the revenue engine — getting CX right from the start maximizes the long-term ROI of the entire Oracle investment
  • Customer Master cleansing happens once, for CX, and is inherited by ERP — eliminates dual data cleansing effort
  • CPQ-defined fee structures inform ERP Project Contract billing rule design — creating internal consistency between how RS&H prices work (CPQ) and how it bills clients (Oracle PPM)
  • BD team and Marketing are typically the most enthusiastic Oracle adopters — starting with CX builds organizational momentum and a "wins" narrative that helps sustain the longer ERP/EPM effort
  • Oracle Eloqua's marketing automation capability can be activated and delivering ROI (client campaigns, pipeline nurture) while ERP build is still underway

CX-First — Risks & Mitigations

  • Risk: CX data model decisions (customer hierarchy, account structure) constrain ERP AR design; if Finance disagrees with CX-led customer design late in the program, expensive rework results
    Mitigation: Joint CX+Finance Customer Master Design Workshop in Month 1; Finance VP and BD Director must co-sign customer model before Month 3
  • Risk: CPQ complexity is frequently underestimated; AEC fee structures with multiple billing types, teaming agreements, and government rate schedules can extend CPQ configuration well beyond initial estimates
    Mitigation: CPQ scoping workshop in Month 1; fixed-fee CPQ contract with defined rule-count ceiling; consider phasing CPQ complexity (basic T&M/lump sum at go-live; government fee schedules in Phase 2)
  • Risk: BD staff adoption of CRM is the industry's hardest change management challenge in AEC — building the platform first does not guarantee adoption
    Mitigation: Executive mandate required; BD Director compensation tied to pipeline data quality in Oracle Sales Cloud; super-user champions in each major office
  • Risk: CX and ERP competing for the same integration team during the build phase
    Mitigation: Dedicated integration sub-team (Broadpin recommended) with CX and ERP integration tracks running in parallel under separate leads

A2: ERP-First Big Bang

Philosophy: Within the Big Bang simultaneous go-live, ERP (Oracle Financials + PPM) drives all foundational architecture decisions — Chart of Accounts, Customer Master, Project Structure, Legal Entity design. ERP-First — Foundational Sequencing

  • Month 1–3 (ERP only): Chart of Accounts design, Legal Entity structure, Business Unit hierarchy, Project type and WBS template library — all finalized before EPM and CX design begin. This is the critical path gating item for the entire Big Bang.
  • Month 4–6 (ERP leads; EPM/CX design begins): Once COA is baselined, EPM planning dimension design begins (PBCS chart of accounts must mirror ERP COA exactly). CX Account/Customer hierarchy design begins referencing ERP AR customer segments.
  • Oracle Project Contracts (ERP) → CPQ (CX): ERP Project Contract billing rule library is defined first; Oracle CPQ fee configuration inherits these billing structures — ensuring CX proposal pricing and ERP project billing are identical
  • Payroll Integration (Month 2): ADP/Payroll → Oracle PPM labor costing integration is designed first; this is Day-1 critical and cannot be delayed by CX or EPM design activities
  • Security & Roles (ERP-first): Oracle Cloud security architecture begins with ERP roles (400+ duty roles for Finance/Projects); CX and EPM roles are added later but must be compatible with the ERP security model
  • Data Migration (ERP-led): Legacy project data, customer master, COA mapping — all data migration strategy is driven by ERP requirements; EPM and CX data cleansing is secondary and subordinate to ERP migration timeline

ERP-First — Advantages

  • ERP COA design is the foundational dependency for every other Oracle module — getting it right first, before EPM and CX design are underway, eliminates the most common cause of rework in Big Bang programs
  • Project structure and billing rules are the most AEC-specific configuration in the entire Oracle suite; ERP-first ensures the SI's most experienced AEC Oracle PPM resource is fully focused on this workstream before multi-module complexity begins
  • Labor costing and payroll integration — Day-1 critical systems — get maximum design attention before EPM workforce planning and CX resource scheduling considerations are introduced
  • ERP data migration (the most complex data migration task) is not competing for bandwidth with CX customer data cleansing or EPM historical actuals loading
  • Consistent with Oracle's own recommended implementation sequence for project-based professional services companies

ERP-First Big Bang — Risks

  • Risk: 6–8 week delay before EPM and CX design begins compresses their build windows; if ERP design runs long, EPM and CX build phases are squeezed
    Mitigation: Hard COA design deadline at Month 3; COA Design Committee with weekly decision cadence from Day 1
  • Risk: CX team (BD Directors, Marketing) experiences a 6–8 week period where they have no visible Oracle activity — organizational enthusiasm can wane
    Mitigation: Use the ERP-design delay period for CX OCM activities — BD stakeholder mapping, opportunity process redesign workshops, Sales Cloud demo sessions; keep CX stakeholders engaged without starting formal design
  • Risk: EPM planning dimensions locked to ERP COA — any post-Month-3 COA change causes EPM rework
    Mitigation: COA freeze date is a hard governance commitment; any COA change after Month 3 requires Executive Sponsor approval and a formal EPM impact assessment

A3: EPM-First Big Bang + Other Considerations

Philosophy: Oracle EPM (PBCS, FCCS) drives the enterprise data model — the EPM planning dimensions, COA hierarchy, entity structure, and management reporting framework are defined first, and both ERP and CX must conform to the EPM-defined structure. Finance leadership sets the data model; operational systems follow.

Best For: Organizations where the CFO/Board has an overriding mandate for management reporting transformation and planning governance — where the primary dissatisfaction is "we can't see what's happening financially" rather than "our project systems are broken"

EPM-First — Design Sequence & Prerequisites

  • Month 1–3: EPM Dimension Design Workshop (lead activity): Define the EPM Account dimension (financial COA), Entity dimension (legal entities + management units), Project dimension (project hierarchy for planning), Scenario dimension (Budget, Latest Estimate, Actuals, Prior Year). This EPM dimension design becomes the ERP COA design — Finance leadership must treat this as a combined EPM+ERP architecture decision, not separate activities.
  • Management Reporting Framework First: Define the P&L, Balance Sheet, and Cash Flow reporting hierarchy in EPM Narrative Reporting before ERP GL segments are configured; ERP GL is then built to support pre-defined management report structures — not the other way around
  • FCCS Entity Hierarchy (Month 2): Multi-entity consolidation structure in FCCS is defined before ERP Legal Entity and Business Unit configuration begins; eliminates the common problem of ERP Business Unit design that doesn't consolidate cleanly in FCCS
  • Budget & Forecast Process Reengineering (Month 1–4): EPM-first approach includes a parallel Finance process redesign workstream — budget cycle redesign, reforecast cadence, management reporting calendar — so the Oracle PBCS configuration reflects a redesigned process, not the current spreadsheet-based process in a new tool
  • CX Pipeline Integration into EPM (Month 5+): Once PBCS planning model is designed, the CX Sales Cloud pipeline probability model is defined to feed PBCS revenue forecasting — CX opportunity stages and probability percentages map to EPM revenue planning assumptions

EPM-First — Strategic Advantages

  • Management reporting and planning requirements define the data model — the most common Oracle failure mode (ERP COA that doesn't support management reporting needs) is eliminated by design
  • Board and Executive Leadership see the clearest, most immediate financial reporting benefits as the EPM model is the first thing fully designed and validated — builds confidence in the program
  • FCCS entity and intercompany elimination logic is defined before ERP legal entity configuration — prevents the expensive scenario of discovering FCCS can't consolidate the ERP entities as configured
  • Budget cycle improvement (replacing spreadsheet budgeting) is the highest-ROI, fastest-to-realize benefit in the Oracle EPM suite; EPM-first ensures this benefit is captured at go-live rather than 18 months later
  • Workforce planning model (headcount-driven cost planning in PBCS) is designed to align with ERP labor costing categories from the start — creating a coherent actual-vs-plan comparison from Day 1 of production

EPM-First — Risks & Mitigations

  • Risk: EPM dimension design is highly abstract at the start of a program — business users struggle to evaluate dimension hierarchies without seeing ERP actuals flowing through them
    Mitigation: Use legacy GL data extracts to prototype EPM planning model in PBCS sandbox during design phase; validate dimensions against real financial data before committing to the model
  • Risk: EPM-defined COA may prioritize management reporting at the expense of operational ERP requirements (transaction-level detail, project-level cost tracking)
    Mitigation: Joint COA Design Committee with EPM lead (Finance), ERP lead (Controller/Project Accounting), and Technology/Data Architect; all three sign off before freeze; use Oracle's "EPM-ERP COA Alignment Workbook" accelerator
  • Risk: EPM planning model is sophisticated — Huron or equivalent EPM specialist required from Day 1; if primary SI lacks EPM depth, EPM-first approach amplifies the gap
    Mitigation: Huron engaged as EPM design lead from Month 1; primary SI handles ERP/CX tracks; EPM and ERP design leads meet weekly to align dimensional decisions
  • Risk: CX pipeline-to-EPM-forecast integration is the newest and least-proven Oracle integration pattern; limited reference implementations
    Mitigation: Oracle Success Navigator team engaged to validate integration approach; Broadpin engaged for OIC integration design between CX and PBCS

Other Big Bang Considerations

  • Oracle Quarterly Updates: In a Big Bang program, all three pillars are in active build simultaneously when Oracle's quarterly update is applied; the update must be regression-tested across ERP, EPM, and CX simultaneously — 3x the testing effort vs. a phased approach. Build quarterly update regression testing into the program plan and SI contract from Day 1.
  • Environment Strategy: Big Bang requires a full 4-environment stack (DEV/TEST/UAT/PROD) for all three pillars simultaneously — Oracle Cloud environment costs and administration overhead are higher than phased. Confirm Oracle tenant provisioning timeline with Oracle Cloud Infrastructure team before Month 1.
  • Oracle Fusion Analytics Warehouse (FAW): In a Big Bang scenario, FAW can be activated at go-live with all three pillars feeding it simultaneously — the cross-pillar analytics value (pipeline-to-project-to-financial-outcome in one dashboard) is realized from Day 1, not after multiple waves. This is the strongest analytical argument for Big Bang.
  • SI Team Sizing: Big Bang requires the SI to field simultaneous ERP, EPM, and CX workstreams — a much larger SI team than phased. Ensure the SI's staffing plan is realistic and that key functional leads are not double-booked across workstreams. Request org charts for the proposed SI team as part of the RFP response.
  • Budget Contingency: Big Bang programs have higher scope creep risk — three interdependent workstreams means one module's change request often triggers cascading changes in the others. Recommend 15–20% contingency reserve vs. 10–12% for phased approach.

Scenario B: ERP → EPM → CX — Three Sequential Waves ⭐ Recommended

Summary: The dependency-respecting, lowest-risk phased approach for a project-centric AEC firm. ERP (Financials, PPM, Procurement) stabilizes the operational core in Wave 1. EPM (PBCS, FCCS, ARCS) builds the planning and reporting layer on proven ERP actuals in Wave 2. CX (Sales, Service, CPQ) completes the enterprise suite in Wave 3 with a functioning project system to receive the handoff.

Three delivery sub-approaches exist within Scenario B — differing in how much wave overlap is permitted, how SI resources are structured, and how aggressively the timeline is compressed between waves.

B1: Sequential Clean Breaks — Classic Three-Wave

Philosophy: Each wave fully completes — including stabilization — before the next wave's design begins. No concurrent workstreams. The SI team pivots completely from ERP to EPM at the Wave 1 close; from EPM to CX at the Wave 2 close. Maximum knowledge transfer; minimum parallel complexity.

Total Timeline: 32–36 months | Wave 1 ERP: Mo 1–16 | Stabilization: Mo 15–18 | Wave 2 EPM: Mo 17–26 | Stabilization: Mo 25–28 | Wave 3 CX: Mo 27–34

Best For: Organizations with limited internal program management capacity; where the risk of parallel complexity outweighs the cost of a longer total timeline; first Oracle Cloud implementation with no prior cloud ERP experience

  • Stabilization 1 (Mo 15–18): Hypercare support, defect resolution, OTBI report refinements, super user network activated, Oracle quarterly update regression test (if applicable), EPM design kickoff in final 2 weeks of stabilization

  • Wave 2 (EPM) Exit Criteria: 1 full budget cycle completed in PBCS, FCCS consolidation validated against legacy, ARCS rec completion rate ≥90%, Narrative Reporting board package produced for one reporting period

B1 — Advantages

  • ERP stabilization period allows RS&H's COE to build genuine operational independence before the next wave adds complexity
  • EPM design begins with 3+ months of real Oracle ERP actuals — dramatically better planning model quality than EPM seeded with legacy data

Scenario C: ERP + CX Concurrent → EPM

Summary: ERP and CX are deployed simultaneously in Wave 1, leveraging their shared customer/account data model dependency. EPM follows in Wave 2 once a full year of ERP actuals is available to seed the planning model. Two waves total.

Three sub-approaches address the most critical design decision in Scenario C: which pillar — ERP or CX — drives the shared data model, and how the concurrent workstreams are organized to avoid the integration conflicts that typically derail concurrent deployments.

C1: ERP-Led Concurrent

Philosophy: ERP design runs 6–8 weeks ahead of CX. The Oracle ERP team finalizes the COA, Legal Entity structure, Customer Master model, and AR customer segmentation first. CX Sales Cloud then inherits the ERP-defined customer/account hierarchy. The shared data model is owned by ERP Finance; CX BD team adapts.

Timeline: Wave 1 (ERP+CX): Mo 1–20 | Wave 2 (EPM): Mo 19–28 | Total: ~30 months

Best For: Organizations where Finance/Operations authority is stronger than BD authority; where Oracle PPM project billing accuracy is the primary pain point and CX pipeline visibility is a secondary (but important) benefit

ERP-Led Data Model — Customer Master Architecture

  • Month 1–3 (ERP leads): Oracle AR Customer Master model is defined — customer account segments, parent/child client hierarchy, credit terms, payment methods. CX Account/Contact model is then mapped to the AR Customer model in Month 3–4.
  • Customer Segmentation Authority: Finance defines customer segments (e.g., Federal, State/Local, Private, International) for AR reporting purposes first; CX then uses these same segments as the Account Type dimension in Sales Cloud — ensuring AR analytics and CX pipeline analytics use identical client categorizations
  • Project Contract → CPQ Sequence: Oracle PPM Project Contract billing rule types are defined in ERP first (T&M, lump sum, cost-plus); CPQ is then configured to produce fee proposals that map to these billing types — preventing the common error where CPQ pricing structures don't match ERP billing configurations
  • Integration Design (Month 4): ERP-to-CX integration (Customer Master sync, AR→CX payment status, Project Contract→CX opportunity status) is architected after ERP data model is baselined; Broadpin leads this OIC design
  • CX Opportunity→Project Handoff (Month 6): CX award event triggers Oracle PPM Project Initiation; handoff data mapping is designed using the ERP project template structure finalized in Month 3

C1 — Advantages & Risks

  • Advantage: ERP's authoritative customer model eliminates the dual-maintenance customer record problem that plagues AEC firms with separate ERP and CRM systems; one master, one cleansing exercise
  • Advantage: CPQ fee structures inherit ERP billing types — proposal pricing and project billing are architecturally aligned from Day 1, enabling automated fee validation before contract execution
  • Advantage: EPM Wave 2 benefits from both ERP actuals AND CX pipeline data — PBCS revenue forecasting can use probability-weighted pipeline from Sales Cloud, the highest-quality EPM input possible
  • Risk: BD team's CX design is blocked for 6–8 weeks while ERP customer model is defined — BD stakeholder impatience

    Mitigation: Joint Customer Master Design Workshop in Month 2 with Finance VP + BD Director + Marketing; define dual-purpose segment structure that serves both AR reporting and CX pipeline analytics

Philosophy: Deploy only Oracle PBCS (Planning and Budgeting) in Wave 1 — the minimum EPM scope needed to replace spreadsheet budgeting and enable real-time reforecasting. Legacy Data Bridge Architecture

  • Legacy ERP Extract (FDMEE): Monthly GL trial balance, headcount file, and project summary data extracted from legacy ERP → Oracle Data Management (FDMEE) → loaded to PBCS as prior-period actuals. This bridge must be designed to be replaced, not made permanent.
  • PBCS COA = Future Oracle ERP COA: Unlike D1, in D3 the PBCS Account dimension is designed from the start to match the future Oracle ERP COA (not the current legacy COA). FDMEE mapping tables translate legacy COA to new COA at load time — this is extra complexity in the bridge but eliminates Wave 2 EPM rework entirely.
  • Concurrent ERP Design (Month 6–8 start): ERP design begins during the EPM build phase (Month 6–8), following Scenario B2's overlapping wave model. ERP COA design is validated against the PBCS Account dimension to confirm compatibility before ERP COA is frozen.
  • Bridge Retirement Trigger: When Oracle ERP goes live, a one-time restatement reconciliation is run (FDMEE-loaded legacy actuals vs. Oracle ERP actuals for the overlap period), the FDMEE bridge is retired, and Oracle native ERP→PBCS integration (OIC or direct FDMEE) takes over permanently.
  • FCCS Parallel Run: FCCS consolidation runs in parallel using legacy entity data for the bridge period; first fully native FCCS consolidation occurs at Month 23–24 post-ERP go-live.

Oracle CX in an AEC Context

CX (Customer Experience) in AEC is fundamentally about Business Development pipeline management and client relationship continuity. Unlike a product company where CX manages thousands of transactions, an AEC firm's CX platform manages a portfolio of 50–200 active opportunities at any time, each potentially worth millions. The Oracle CX platform provides the pipeline visibility, pursuit tracking, and client intelligence that drives RS&H's revenue growth — and, critically, automates the handoff from a won opportunity to a new Oracle PPM project.

Best Practices — Oracle Fusion Redwood Implementation
Program Governance Best Practices

1. Executive Sponsor Active Engagement

Oracle implementations fail most often due to sponsor disengagement after kickoff. Best practice: Executive Sponsor (CFO or COO) attends monthly Steering Committee, reviews go/no-go criteria personally, and communicates priority to the organization. At firms where the sponsor delegated entirely to the PMO, defect resolution time increased 3x due to lack of business decision authority.

2. Freeze the Scope — Use Change Control Rigorously

The #1 cost driver in Oracle overruns is scope creep — "while we're in there" additions requested by business users during UAT. Best practice: formal Change Request Board meets bi-weekly; any scope addition requires cost/timeline impact analysis before approval. All scope changes require CTO/CFO/Sponsor sign-off above a defined threshold. The SI must present all CRs with a "defer to Phase 2" alternative.

3. Data Migration: Start Day 1, Never Late

Data migration is consistently underestimated in Oracle programs. Best practice: appoint a dedicated Data Migration Lead on Day 1, run mock data loads monthly starting in Month 3. The Chart of Accounts must be 90% finalized before any data migration templates are developed. Legacy data quality analysis (profiling) should happen in the first 60 days — surprises found late in the program are catastrophic to timelines.

4. Oracle's "Adopt Standard" Principle

Oracle's delivered functionality covers 85–90% of AEC requirements. The most expensive programs are those that customize Oracle to match legacy processes rather than re-engineering processes to use Oracle standard. Best practice: challenge every "we need a custom" request with "what would we need to change in our process to use the standard?" Customizations become technical debt at every quarterly Oracle update.

Technical Best Practices

5. Oracle Integration Cloud (OIC) for All Integrations

Do not use custom middleware or point-to-point API integrations for Oracle Cloud. Use Oracle Integration Cloud as the integration hub — it is purpose-built for Oracle Fusion, has pre-built adapters for 400+ applications, and is supported by Oracle through quarterly updates. Firms that used MuleSoft or TIBCO for Oracle integrations incur higher ongoing maintenance costs and break more frequently at quarterly updates.

6. Quarterly Update Strategy — Critical for AEC

Oracle releases quarterly updates (January, April, July, October) that are automatically applied to all cloud tenants. Best practice: establish a Quarterly Update Testing protocol — test all critical business processes in UAT environment 2 weeks before the update hits production. Assign an Oracle Cloud "Release Manager" role to an Technology team member. Subscribe to Oracle's release documentation service and triage each release note for impact.

7. Redwood Adoption — Don't Fight It

Oracle is sunsetting Classic UI. Do not attempt to delay Redwood adoption or configure the system to retain Classic look-and-feel. Redwood personalization should be embraced — it reduces training time and improves adoption. Best practice: involve end users in Redwood persona design during the Design phase; users who co-design their Redwood homepage adopt the system 40% faster than those who receive a pre-configured layout.

8. Roles & Security — Start Early, Test Often

Oracle Cloud security design is complex — 200+ predefined roles, duty roles, data roles. Best practice: begin role mapping in Month 2 (not Month 8). Run security testing monthly. Ensure Segregation of Duties (SOD) conflicts are resolved before UAT — fixing SoD conflicts discovered during UAT adds 4–8 weeks to timelines. Use Oracle's Cloud Access Security (OCAS) tool for SoD analysis.

OCM Best Practices for AEC Organizations

9. AEC-Specific Training Strategy

AEC professionals are project-focused and have limited patience for classroom training. Best practice: role-based, scenario-based training using actual RS&H project data (anonymized) in the sandbox environment. Training should be no longer than 2-hour sessions; deploy Oracle's Guided Learning (in-application overlay guidance) so users get help in the moment without leaving Oracle. Train 6–8 weeks before go-live, not 6 months before.

10. Super User Network — AEC Field Champion Model

Identify 1–2 "Oracle Champions" in each office/service line who receive advanced training and serve as first-level support post-go-live. This is especially important in AEC where offices are geographically distributed and Technology cannot be on-site everywhere. Champions reduce helpdesk volume by 30–50% in the first 6 months. Champions should receive recognition (title, bonus, conference access) for the role.

Risk Detail & Mitigation Strategies

Risk Assessment Methodology

Each risk is assessed on two dimensions: Likelihood (how probable is this risk given RS&H's current organizational state) and Impact (what is the cost/schedule/quality consequence if this risk materializes). Risks are rated High/Medium/Low and mitigation strategies are defined for each. This register should be reviewed monthly by the Steering Committee.

Capacity & Availability Risks

RISK R-01: Controller/Finance Lead Overloaded — HIGH

Description: The most experienced Finance SME (typically Controller or Senior Accountant) is expected to simultaneously manage BAU period close, year-end audit support, and Oracle implementation design activities. This is the most commonly cited root cause of AEC Oracle program delays.

Likelihood: Very High (observed in 80%+ of AEC implementations) | Impact: High — design quality suffers, decision delays cascade to build team

Mitigations:

  • Hire contract Controller support for 12–18 months to manage BAU close while Controller focuses on Oracle
  • Define formal "protected time" calendar: Controller owns Oracle Tue/Wed/Thu; BAU on Mon/Fri
  • Designate a Deputy Controller-equivalent who can run routine close tasks independently
  • Reduce scope of monthly BAU close activities during implementation (defer non-critical analysis)

RISK R-02: Single Technology Architect Dependency — HIGH

Description: RS&H's Enterprise Architect is likely the only person who understands the full legacy integration landscape (which systems send data to the ERP, what the data formats are, where the technical debt lives). If this person leaves during the program or is not fully committed, integration design is critically delayed.

Likelihood: Medium-High | Impact: Very High — integration failures at go-live are catastrophic

Mitigations:

  • Create a formal Integration Knowledge Base during Months 1–3 — document every system, API, data format, and business rule before the program proceeds
  • Identify and cross-train a secondary Technology integration resource as backup on all integration work
  • SI integration team must shadow the EA and document findings independently — no single source of truth in one person's head
  • Retention bonus or role elevation for EA during program duration

RISK R-03: Project Billing SME Availability During Peak Billing Periods — HIGH (AEC-Specific)

Description: Project Billing is the highest-complexity Oracle PPM configuration area for AEC. The SME required to design billing rules (T&M, lump sum, cost-plus, milestone) is the same person who runs the current billing cycle. At month-end, this SME is 100% consumed by current billing — unavailable for Oracle configuration reviews or UAT.

Likelihood: High | Impact: High — billing configuration errors discovered late are expensive to remediate

Mitigations:

  • Hire a contract Billing Coordinator to run current billing under the SME's supervision
  • Schedule all Oracle billing design workshops in the first 2 weeks of each month (post-billing cycle)
  • Engage Oracle Consulting's AEC Billing specialist to supplement SI team on PPM billing configuration

RISK R-04: OCM Under-Investment — MEDIUM

Description: AEC firms historically underinvest in organizational change management, treating it as "training" rather than a strategic program workstream. Without structured OCM, user adoption post-go-live is poor and the expected productivity/efficiency gains are not realized.

Mitigation: Fund OCM at 12–15% of total SI cost (industry best practice); hire a dedicated OCM Lead with Oracle implementation experience; OCM reports to the Executive Sponsor, not to Technology.

RISK R-05: Chart of Accounts Design Delay — MEDIUM

Description: The COA design is the longest lead-time item on the critical path. It requires alignment across Finance, Tax, Operations, and Technology. Disagreements about segment structure (particularly the project segment and cost center hierarchy) can take months to resolve. Any COA change after the EPM data model is built is extremely expensive.

Mitigation: Assign COA Design as a Week-1 activity with a dedicated COA Design Committee (CFO, Controller, Tax, Technology). Set a hard decision deadline of Month 3 for COA finalization. Use Oracle's COA Design Accelerator tool to model different structures.

RISK R-06: Hackett Transition Gap — MEDIUM

Description: The 7-week scope extension completes five planning workstreams. If the primary SI engagement doesn't start within 60–90 days of Hackett completion, momentum is lost, personnel change, and the planning artifacts become stale. There is also a knowledge transfer risk — insights discovered during the Hackett phase must be formally transferred to the implementation SI.

Mitigation: Begin SI RFP/selection process no later than Month –2 of Hackett extension. Target SI contract execution within 30 days of Hackett engagement end. Hackett produces formal Knowledge Transfer Document as a final deliverable.

RISK R-07: Oracle Quarterly Update Disruption — LOW-MEDIUM

Description: Oracle's quarterly updates are automatic. A quarterly update applied to the production environment mid-implementation can change behavior of configured features. This is a known risk but manageable with the right process.

Mitigation: Establish Quarterly Update Review Board. Test all quarterly updates in UAT 2 weeks before production application. SI contract must include quarterly update regression testing as an in-scope activity for all waves.

  • Cons: Higher long-term cost, slower response than internal team, knowledge drain from RS&H, SI dependency

Recommendation: Hybrid model — internal COE for ERP/EPM (higher business knowledge dependency), AMS for CX (more technical, lower BAU interaction)

OIC & OCI — Oracle Platform Layer

Why the Platform Layer Is a First-Class Program Concern

Oracle Integration Cloud (OIC) and Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) are not afterthoughts in an Oracle Fusion deployment — they are the connective tissue that determines whether the ERP, EPM, and CX pillars function as an integrated enterprise platform or as three disconnected SaaS applications. RS&H's integration landscape (ADP payroll, banking, legacy project systems, future HRIS, downstream analytics) requires OIC to be architected as a strategic asset from Day 1, not bolted on as an afterthought in Month 9. OCI provides the infrastructure and security backbone for all Oracle Cloud services, including identity management (IDCS/IAM), tenant configuration, data sovereignty, and disaster recovery.

Oracle Integration Cloud (OIC) — Architecture for RS&H

What OIC Does in an Oracle Fusion Program

OIC is Oracle's cloud-native integration platform — a pre-built, managed middleware layer that connects Oracle Fusion applications to each other and to non-Oracle systems. Unlike on-premise middleware (MuleSoft, TIBCO, WebMethods), OIC is fully managed by Oracle, scales automatically, and is updated with Oracle's quarterly releases. For RS&H, OIC is the sole recommended integration technology for all ERP, EPM, and CX integrations.

  • Pre-built Adapters: OIC ships with 400+ pre-built adapters including Oracle Fusion ERP, HCM, EPM, CX, ADP, Workday, Salesforce, ServiceNow, SAP, NetSuite, and major banking formats (BAI2, ISO 20022, SWIFT). RS&H's ADP and banking integrations use certified pre-built adapters — not custom code.
  • Visual Designer: OIC's integration flows are designed in a drag-and-drop visual canvas — no Java or XML coding required for standard patterns. RS&H's Technology team can maintain and modify integrations without SI dependency after COE training.
  • Monitoring Dashboard: All integration flows are monitored in OIC's real-time dashboard — failed messages, reprocessing, throughput metrics, error rate trending. This replaces the "check the email inbox for bounce notifications" approach common in legacy middleware.
  • Process Automation: OIC's Process Cloud module enables human-in-the-loop workflows — approval routing, exception management, escalation — without custom development. Relevant for RS&H's PO approval, project initiation, and contract modification workflows.

OIC Integration Catalog — RS&H Full Program

OIC Design Principles — RS&H

Hub-and-Spoke Architecture

All integrations route through OIC as the central hub — no point-to-point connections between Oracle applications and non-Oracle systems. This means there is one place to monitor all integrations, one place to apply security policies, and one place to fix a broken connection. Point-to-point integrations built outside OIC create invisible dependencies that break silently.

Event-Driven over Batch (Where Possible)

Oracle Fusion publishes Business Events via Oracle Event Catalog — when an invoice is approved, a project is awarded, or a payment is processed, an event fires that OIC can consume in near-real-time. Event-driven integration (CX award → PPM project in seconds) delivers a fundamentally better user experience than overnight batch jobs and eliminates the "it won't show up until tomorrow" problem common in legacy ERP integrations.

Error Handling as a First-Class Requirement

Every OIC integration flow must have defined error handling: what happens if the ADP payload has an unrecognized project code? What happens if the bank rejects a payment file? OIC's built-in error framework supports automatic retry, alert routing to a named DL, and manual reprocessing from the monitoring console. RS&H's Technology COE must own the OIC monitoring dashboard and error response runbook from Day 1 of production.

Idempotency — Avoid Duplicate Processing

OIC integrations must be designed to be idempotent — running the same integration twice must not create duplicate records (e.g., duplicate project cost entries if a payroll file is reprocessed). Oracle recommends using Oracle ERP's "External Source Reference" field to track processed records and reject duplicates. This is a Wave 1 design requirement, not a Wave 3 fix.

Broadpin as OIC Authority

Broadpin's OIC specialization positions them as the recommended OIC design and build authority across all waves. Their role as a neutral sub-contractor (not affiliated with the primary ERP or CX SI) prevents the common failure mode where two SI teams build conflicting OIC integration architectures. Broadpin owns all OIC flows; SI teams consume integration services via documented APIs.

Quarterly Update Impact on OIC

Oracle's quarterly updates can change Oracle Fusion REST APIs, business event schemas, and adapter connection parameters. OIC integrations must be regression-tested as part of every quarterly update cycle — this is Broadpin's or the OIC COE team's responsibility. A single broken integration at a quarterly update boundary can silently corrupt project costing data for a full pay period before anyone notices.

Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) — RS&H Tenant Architecture

OCI Fundamentals for Oracle Fusion

OCI is the infrastructure layer beneath all Oracle Cloud applications. For Oracle Fusion SaaS customers like RS&H, OCI is largely transparent — Oracle manages the infrastructure. However, several OCI capabilities are directly relevant to RS&H's program and require deliberate configuration decisions:

  • Oracle Tenancy: RS&H's Oracle Cloud environment lives in a single Oracle tenancy — a logically isolated cloud partition. All Oracle Fusion applications (ERP, EPM, CX), OIC, OAC, and FAW share the RS&H tenancy. Tenancy design (compartments, policies, network topology) is a Day-1 Technology architecture decision.
  • Region Selection: Oracle Cloud US East (Ashburn, VA) is the primary recommended region for RS&H — proximity to RS&H's distributed US footprint, lowest latency for most associates, FEDRAMP-authorized for any federal contract compliance requirements. US West (Phoenix) as disaster recovery.
  • Data Residency: All Oracle Fusion SaaS data for RS&H resides in Oracle's US data centers — relevant for any federal contract data handling requirements. Oracle provides a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) specifying data residency guarantees.
  • Oracle Autonomous Database: Oracle Fusion Analytics Warehouse (FAW) uses OCI's Autonomous Data Warehouse (ADW) — a self-managing, self-securing analytical database that requires zero DBA administration. RS&H's Technology team does not need to manage the FAW database infrastructure.

OCI Identity & Access Management — Oracle IDCS/IAM

Oracle Identity Cloud Service (IDCS), now part of Oracle IAM, is the identity and SSO layer for all Oracle Cloud applications. RS&H's IDCS configuration is a critical Wave 1 deliverable — users cannot log in to Oracle Fusion without it.

  • Single Sign-On (SSO): IDCS integrates with RS&H's existing identity provider (Microsoft Azure AD / Entra ID is the most common in AEC firms). Associates use their existing RS&H credentials to log in to Oracle Fusion — no separate Oracle password. SAML 2.0 or OIDC federation protocol.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Oracle recommends MFA for all Oracle Cloud access. IDCS supports Microsoft Authenticator, TOTP apps, and email OTP. MFA policy should be configured in IDCS before any users are provisioned in production.
  • Role Provisioning: Oracle Fusion roles are assigned to users via IDCS. Role provisioning can be manual (IDCS admin console), automated via Oracle Human Capital Management role assignment rules, or integrated with RS&H's HR system (Workday, ADP, etc.) for joiner/mover/leaver automation.
  • JIT (Just-in-Time) Provisioning: IDCS supports JIT provisioning — the first time an authorized RS&H user attempts to log in, their Oracle account is auto-created from the HR system record. Eliminates manual account creation for 2,000 associates.
  • Privileged Access: Oracle Cloud Console (OCI) administrative access uses separate privileged accounts with Privileged Access Management (PAM) controls. RS&H's Technology Security policy for privileged cloud access should be defined before production provisioning.

OCI Security Architecture — Key Controls for RS&H

Oracle Cloud Security Shared Responsibility Model

Oracle and RS&H share security responsibility in Oracle Cloud. Understanding the boundary is critical:

  • Oracle's Responsibility: Physical data center security, network infrastructure, hypervisor/compute/storage security, Oracle application security patches (quarterly updates), Oracle database security, data center SOC 2 Type II / ISO 27001 compliance
  • RS&H's Responsibility: Oracle Fusion role and access configuration, Segregation of Duties (SoD) compliance, IDCS/IAM identity governance, data classification and handling within Oracle, API key management, OIC integration security (TLS, OAuth tokens), OCI compartment policies, privileged access management for Oracle admin accounts
  • Shared: Data encryption (Oracle encrypts at rest and in transit; RS&H configures key management preferences), MFA enforcement, incident response (Oracle detects infrastructure threats; RS&H detects application-layer threats)

Key Security Configurations — Wave 1 Deliverables

  • Oracle Cloud Access Security (OCAS): Oracle's SoD analysis tool — scans all Oracle Fusion role assignments for segregation of duties conflicts (e.g., same user can create and approve a payment). Must be run before UAT sign-off; all High-severity SoD conflicts resolved before go-live.
  • Data Masking: Production data used in UAT/TEST environments must be masked — employee names, SSNs, bank account numbers, client contract values. Oracle provides Data Safe (OCI service) for automated data masking; configure before first data clone.
  • API Security: All OIC integration connections use OAuth 2.0 client credentials flow — no basic auth, no hardcoded passwords. API secrets stored in OCI Vault; rotated annually. Broadpin must comply with RS&H's API security standards in all OIC flow designs.
  • Audit Logging: Oracle Fusion Audit Log captures all configuration changes, data access, and security events. Enable comprehensive audit logging on Day 1; configure audit log retention per RS&H's records management policy. Critical for SOX compliance and Technology security incident investigation.
  • Oracle Cloud Guard: OCI-native security posture management tool — continuously monitors RS&H's Oracle Cloud tenancy for misconfigurations, exposed resources, and anomalous activity. Activate from Day 1 at no additional cost (included in OCI).
  • Vulnerability Scanning: Oracle Cloud Infrastructure Vulnerability Scanning scans RS&H's OCI compute and network configurations. Relevant if RS&H hosts any custom components (Oracle APEX apps, OCI VMs) in the tenancy.

Rate Hold Considerations

  • Oracle UCM unit rates are typically held for the initial contract term (usually 1–3 years). Confirm contract anniversary date with Oracle Customer Success.
  • B87366 / B82309 (Gov SKUs): US Government cloud pricing is subject to Oracle's OCI Gov terms and is typically reviewed annually. Confirm whether FedRAMP moderate or high authorization applies to RS&H's contract scope.
  • Uplift at renewal: Oracle standard uplift is 3–5% annually. Negotiate a multi-year rate lock (3 years) at contract renewal to protect against uplift on high-volume SKUs (ERP Self-Service, HCM Base).
  • AI Agent SKUs (B112535–B112538): New SKU class with list pricing only — no contracted rate in Table 2. Negotiate contracted rate before activating; list pricing represents worst case.

  • ERP Self-Service (B91080): At 1,900 employees × $3.40, confirm all 1,900 actually need Self-Service access on Day 1. A phased ramp (start with 1,000, expand after training) may reduce Year 1 cost by ~$38K.

Best Practice Guidelines

Adopt, Don't Adapt

The single highest-value configuration discipline is resisting customization. Each customization adds: (1) implementation cost, (2) regression testing burden on every quarterly Oracle update, and (3) upgrade risk. AEC firms that hold customizations below 15% of config see 40% lower Year 2–5 maintenance costs. Every exception must go to the Change Control Board with a cost estimate.

Data Migration Ownership

Do not let the SI own data migration. SI firms routinely under-scope data migration because it's your data, not theirs. RS&H must appoint a dedicated Data Migration Lead with protected capacity from Day 1. The Vision → Oracle data mapping is the most RS&H-specific work in the program — no SI can do it without deep RS&H involvement.

Fixed-Price Wave 1 Contract

The SI contract for Wave 1 should be fixed-price for design/build with clearly defined RS&H SME availability commitments. Time-and-materials SI contracts for ERP implementation create perverse incentives. Scope change requests (PCRs) must route through Program Director and be approved by Steering Committee before SI work begins.

OCM as a Dedicated Workstream

Change management budgeted below 10% of total program cost is a red flag. RS&H's finance function will experience the largest day-one change — replacing 15+ years of Deltek workflows. OCM must be a named workstream with a dedicated lead, not embedded in PMO. Super-user network of 50+ people minimum; training completion rate ≥85% is a go/no-go criterion.
